Photography, and being present

Justen Bristlin March 13, 2026

A few months ago I picked up a screenless camera - a Camp Snap.

No display. No preview. No instant feedback.

At first that feels… wrong. Modern cameras and phones train you to take a photo and immediately check it. Then take another. Zoom in. Adjust. Repeat until it’s perfect. Photography becomes this loop of capture → review → tweak → capture again.

The Camp Snap breaks that loop completely.

When I’m out doing something walking around, meeting friends, going somewhere interesting, I can still take photos. But the difference is I don’t stop being there. There’s no flipping the camera around to check the shot. No standing in place reviewing the last ten pictures. No getting pulled into the tiny editing studio that lives inside my pocket computer.

You just… take the photo and keep going.

Sometimes the results are messy. Blurry shots. Weird framing. That’s part of the deal.

But something interesting happens when you remove the ability to check your work instantly: you start taking photos of what you're actually experiencing, not what will look best on a screen. Just capturing moments that feel worth remembering.

Later, when I finally pull the photos off the camera, it’s almost like opening a time capsule. I see the walk, the outing, the random things that caught my attention but I also remember the experience itself because I wasn’t constantly interrupting it to evaluate the documentation.

Phones have made photography incredibly powerful, but they also quietly turn experiences into a series of micro-reviews. You’re always stepping out of the moment to check the moment.

A camera without a screen removes that temptation.

It reminds me that photography doesn’t have to be about perfection. Sometimes it’s just about leaving a few breadcrumbs behind so your future self can remember where you’ve been.

Plus, it's kind of fun to sit in a taproom with an ipad to review what you shot earlier and do the editing there, with a beer in hand as a whole look back on the day, or shoot maybe a review of the week and you get to re-live Monday on Saturday.
